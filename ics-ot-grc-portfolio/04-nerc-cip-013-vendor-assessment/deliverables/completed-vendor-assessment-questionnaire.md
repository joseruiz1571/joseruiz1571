## Section 2: Software Development and Release Practices

| # | Question | Vendor Response | Assessor Note |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------|
| 2.1 | Describe your software/firmware development lifecycle. Is it based on a recognized secure development framework? | We do not develop firmware. SEL develops and releases all firmware for the relay models we service. Our role is to apply SEL-released firmware to customer relays in accordance with SEL's published update procedures. We do not modify firmware code. | **Satisfactory.** This clarification is important: Relay Logic does not develop firmware — they apply vendor-released firmware. This substantially changes the software development lifecycle question. The relevant supply chain risk is integrity of the firmware image in transit from SEL to the customer site, not Relay Logic's development practices. See 2.3. |
| 2.2 | Do you perform security testing on firmware or software before release? | Not applicable — see 2.1. We verify that the firmware version matches the SEL advisory we are working from and that the file hash matches SEL's published checksum. We do not perform independent security testing on SEL firmware. | **Satisfactory given role clarification.** Hash verification against SEL-published checksums is the appropriate compensating control for an integrator with no development role. Independent security testing of OEM firmware would be outside scope and capability for a 42-person firm. |
| 2.3 | How do you ensure that the firmware delivered to Lone Star has not been modified after your final release? Do you provide cryptographic hash values? | Yes. We provide a SHA-256 hash for each firmware image file we deliver, verified against SEL's published checksum for that release. We transfer firmware images from our internal download (obtained from SEL's secure customer portal) to a dedicated USB drive used exclusively for firmware deployment. The USB drive is maintained under chain-of-custody controls — it is not connected to internet-facing systems after the firmware image is loaded. Hash is documented in our field work order. | **Satisfactory.** Chain-of-custody documentation for the firmware delivery USB is a meaningful control. The practice of maintaining a dedicated, air-gapped firmware transfer device is appropriate for this use case. Lone Star will independently verify the SHA-256 hash against SEL's published checksum upon receipt, prior to deployment. Any discrepancy is a stop-work condition. |
| 2.4 | How is firmware stored and transported prior to delivery? Describe the chain of custody from SEL to the customer facility. | SEL firmware is downloaded by our lead engineer from SEL's authenticated customer portal (two-factor authentication required). Downloaded to a dedicated engineering workstation (not internet-connected during download). Transferred to a dedicated, write-protected USB drive. USB is transported to site by the field engineer. Physical custody of the USB is maintained by the named engineer at all times during transport. Hash is documented in the work order before the USB leaves our office. | **Satisfactory.** Detailed chain-of-custody description. Write-protected USB prevents accidental modification. Two-factor authentication on SEL portal reduces risk of download compromise. Documentation of hash before USB leaves the office is a good practice — it creates a pre-departure verification checkpoint. |
| 2.5 | Do you use any open-source or third-party components in the firmware you deliver? | Not applicable — we do not develop firmware. We do use open-source components in our internal engineering tools (relay settings software, fault report analysis utilities), but those tools are not delivered to customers and are not relevant to this engagement. | **Satisfactory.** The third-party component question is most relevant to firmware developers. Since Relay Logic does not develop firmware, this is correctly scoped to not applicable. SEL's firmware SBOM question is addressed separately in Section 3. |
| 2.6 | How do you notify customers of security vulnerabilities discovered in previously delivered software or firmware? | We notify customers of relevant SEL security advisories when we become aware of them through CISA ICS-CERT or SEL's advisory distribution. We do not have a formal vulnerability notification SLA. We typically notify customers we have worked with in the past 12 months within 2 business days of an advisory that affects equipment at their site. | **Significant finding (S-01).** The absence of a formal vulnerability notification SLA and notification timeline is a gap. "Typically within 2 business days" for customers within the past 12 months is not contractually binding and does not cover equipment deployed beyond 12 months ago. CIP-013 requires Lone Star to have assurance of notification; this needs to be contractually formalized. Required contract term: written notification within 2 business days of any SEL advisory affecting relays deployed at Lone Star sites, regardless of recency of engagement. |

## Section 3: Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) Capabilities

| # | Question | Vendor Response | Assessor Note |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------|
| 3.1 | Can you provide an SBOM for the firmware update to be deployed? | No. We do not produce SBOMs and SEL does not publish one for the SEL-411L firmware. We are not aware of any SBOM for this firmware existing in the industry. | **Significant finding (S-02).** No SBOM means the third-party component composition of the firmware is unknown. The SEL-411L v3.6.2 firmware includes an updated DNP3 handling library (noted in the release advisory). The vulnerability status of that library is unknown without an SBOM. This is a persistent gap in the ICS vendor ecosystem — not unique to Relay Logic. Compensating control: Lone Star will monitor CISA ICS-CERT for any CVE disclosure against SEL-411L v3.6.2 components post-deployment. |
| 3.2 | Can you identify major third-party libraries or components in the firmware? | Unknown. SEL does not publish component-level information for their relay firmware. The v3.6.2 release advisory notes the updated DNP3 handling library but does not identify it by name or version. | **Significant finding (S-02 cont'd).** The updated DNP3 library is specifically relevant given that CVE-2024-LST-0047 on WJSS-001 is also a DNP3 library vulnerability. The possibility that the SEL relay firmware update introduces a differently-vulnerable DNP3 library cannot be independently verified. Monitoring and post-deployment surveillance are the compensating controls. |
| 3.3 | Do you monitor SBOM components for newly disclosed CVEs after firmware delivery? | Not applicable given lack of SBOM capability. We monitor SEL advisories for the relay models we service and will notify Lone Star if a new SEL advisory is issued for the 411L. | **Notable.** Not applicable given SBOM absence. SBOM monitoring would require SBOM existence. SEL advisory monitoring is the practical alternative. |
| 3.4 | Is the firmware solely developed by SEL, or does it incorporate code from other parties? | To our knowledge, firmware is developed by SEL. We are not aware of third-party firmware integrators in the SEL supply chain, but we cannot verify SEL's internal development practices. | **Notable.** Vendor's answer is honest about the limit of their knowledge. Lone Star should consider adding SEL (the manufacturer) to its CIP-013 vendor assessment scope for the next annual review cycle. |
